# Landing Page Strategy: MediPortal

**Date**: 2026-04-07

---

## The Core Challenge

MediPortal's landing page has a specific job: convince a **time-poor, skeptical medical trainee** — someone who's been burned by clunky NHS digital tools — that this app will genuinely save them time. The page needs to do this in under 30 seconds, because that's all the attention you'll get from someone between clinic sessions.

---

## Industry Best Practices Applied

### 1. The "Above the Fold" Rule (Basecamp, Linear, Notion)

The first viewport — before any scrolling — must answer three questions:

- **What is this?** (product category)
- **What does it do for me?** (benefit, not feature)
- **What do I do next?** (CTA)

Best-in-class products nail this in a headline + subheadline + one CTA. Linear does this exceptionally: a sharp headline, a one-line description, and a button. No noise.

**For MediPortal**: The headline shouldn't say "AI-powered portfolio tool." Trainees don't care about AI. They care about time. Something benefit-led like:

> "Your portfolio, done in minutes — not hours."
> Speak about your clinical experience. Get a structured, capability-mapped portfolio entry back.
> [Download on App Store] [Get on Google Play]

### 2. The "Show, Don't Tell" Principle (Loom, Arc, Superhuman)

Medical trainees are visual learners trained on clinical demonstrations. A **product demo video or animated walkthrough** is worth more than 500 words of copy. Superhuman's landing page famously led with a short video showing the product in action.

**For MediPortal**: A 30-45 second looping animation or video showing:

1. Trainee taps record, speaks for 30 seconds about a patient encounter
2. AI processes it (brief loading state)
3. Out comes a structured entry with reflection sections and tagged capabilities
4. Coverage dashboard updates

This single visual sequence communicates the entire value proposition faster than any copy.

### 3. The "Problem-Agitation-Solution" (PAS) Framework

Classic direct-response copywriting pattern used by Basecamp, Hey.com, and most successful SaaS landing pages:

- **Problem**: Name the pain so specifically that the reader thinks "they get me"
- **Agitation**: Make them feel the cost of the status quo
- **Solution**: Present the product as the relief

**For MediPortal**:

- **Problem**: "You became a doctor to help patients, not to spend your evenings writing portfolio entries."
- **Agitation**: "60 minutes per entry. 50+ entries per year. Scrambling before ARCP. Staring at a blank reflection box wondering what to write."
- **Solution**: "MediPortal turns a 3-minute voice note into a complete, curriculum-mapped portfolio entry — with reflections, capabilities, and PDP goals."

This works particularly well for MediPortal because the pain is visceral and universal among the target audience.

### 4. Social Proof Hierarchy (Stripe, Calm, Headspace)

For a medical product, trust is everything. The social proof hierarchy should be:

1. **Peer testimonials** (highest impact) — quotes from real trainees: "I used to spend my Sundays on portfolio. Now I do it on the bus home."
2. **Numbers** — "500+ portfolio entries created" or "Used by trainees across 12 deaneries"
3. **Institutional signals** — "Compliant with GMC data standards" / "UK GDPR compliant" / "PII automatically redacted"
4. **Professional credibility** — "Built by doctors, for doctors" (if applicable)

At MVP/early stage, you may not have all of these. Start with 3-5 beta tester quotes and the compliance badges. Medical trainees trust peers more than marketing.

### 5. The Feature-Benefit Bridge (Apple)

Apple never lists specs without tying them to human outcomes. Every feature section should follow: **Feature -> So What -> So You Can**.

| Don't Say | Say Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| "AI-powered transcription" | "Speak about your case. We'll handle the rest." |
| "Curriculum capability mapping" | "Every entry automatically tagged to your curriculum — no more guessing which capabilities you've covered." |
| "Coverage dashboard" | "See exactly where your portfolio stands, months before ARCP." |
| "PII redaction" | "Patient details are automatically removed. Your portfolio stays compliant without you lifting a finger." |
| "PDP goal generation" | "Actionable development goals that come from your real experiences — not copy-pasted templates." |

### 6. The "One Page, One Job" Principle (Julian Shapiro, Unbounce Research)

Landing pages with a single CTA convert 2-3x better than pages with multiple competing actions. For MediPortal, the single job is: **download the app**.

Every section should funnel toward the same CTA. Don't distract with "learn more," "read our blog," "watch a webinar." The page has one exit: the app store.

Repeat the CTA at least 3 times: above the fold, after the demo/walkthrough section, and at the bottom.

### 7. Objection Handling (Intercom, Notion)

Anticipate and address the top 3-4 objections inline, before the reader has to ask:

- **"Is my data safe?"** — Section on PII redaction, UK GDPR compliance, audio deletion after transcription. This must be prominent — medical data anxiety is real.
- **"Will the AI output actually be good enough?"** — Show a real before/after: raw voice transcript vs. polished portfolio entry. Let the quality speak for itself.
- **"Do I still need to use ePortfolio?"** — Be honest: "MediPortal creates portfolio-ready entries you can transfer to your ePortfolio in seconds." Don't overpromise integration that doesn't exist yet.
- **"Is this just ChatGPT with a wrapper?"** — Emphasize specialty-specific curriculum frameworks, multi-step analysis pipeline, and PII redaction. This is purpose-built, not a generic AI.

### 8. Mobile-First Design (Google's Mobile-First Indexing)

Given MediPortal is a mobile app and trainees will likely discover the landing page on their phones (shared via WhatsApp in trainee group chats), the page must be designed mobile-first:

- Large tap targets for app store buttons
- Vertical-scrolling layout, no horizontal complexity
- Fast loading (under 2 seconds on 4G) — trainees in hospitals often have poor WiFi
- App store badges should be sticky or repeated frequently

### 9. The Specificity Principle (Joanna Wiebe / Copyhackers)

Generic copy kills conversion. The more specific the language, the more the reader trusts that you understand their world. Use the language trainees actually use:

- Say "ARCP" not "annual review"
- Say "CBD" and "mini-CEX" not "assessment types"
- Say "ST2" not "second-year trainee"
- Say "ePortfolio" not "portfolio system"
- Reference "Educational Supervisor meetings" not "review meetings"

This signals insider knowledge and builds immediate trust.

### 10. The "How It Works" Strip (Slack, Dropbox, Duolingo)

A simple 3-step visual strip reduces cognitive load and makes the product feel approachable:

> **Step 1**: Record — Talk through your clinical experience (icon: microphone)
> **Step 2**: Review — AI structures your entry with reflections and capabilities (icon: document with checkmarks)
> **Step 3**: Complete — Finalize and track your coverage (icon: dashboard/progress bar)

Three steps is the magic number. More feels complex. Fewer feels too simple to be real.

## Key Design Principles

- **Color palette**: Medical products benefit from trust colors — blues, whites, with one accent color. Avoid looking like an NHS website (they'll assume it's slow and broken). Look modern like Linear or Notion, but with clinical credibility.
- **Typography**: Clean sans-serif. One font, two weights max. Let whitespace do the work.
- **Imagery**: Phone mockups showing real app screens, not stock photos of doctors with stethoscopes. Trainees see through generic medical imagery instantly.
- **Tone**: Professional but human. Not corporate ("leverage your clinical encounters") and not too casual ("smash your ARCP"). Aim for the tone of a helpful senior registrar.

**Heading**: Built for how trainees actually work.

**Card 2 — Capability mapping**: Every entry automatically tagged to your curriculum. No more guessing which capabilities you've covered or which you're missing.
