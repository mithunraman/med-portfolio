# RCGP Guidelines on AI Use in Portfolio Entries — Detailed Report

_Compiled from official RCGP and GMC sources, March 2026_

---

## 1. RCGP's Overarching Position on AI

The RCGP recognises that AI has the potential to support general practice but requires coordinated, safe rollout across the UK. Their position rejects blanket prohibition of AI — rather than requiring trainees to sign statements affirming they haven't used AI (which risks accidental probity violations), they emphasise that **"the effective, critical use of AI technology will be a skill all GPs need."**

Key statistics from RCGP research (December 2025):

- 28% of GPs currently use AI tools
- Primary uses: clinical documentation (57%), professional development (45%), administrative tasks (44%)
- 89% of non-users and 80% of users cite professional liability as a concern

---

## 2. What Generative AI Is (RCGP Definition)

The RCGP defines generative AI as **"AI systems designed to create new content, such as text, images, music, or video"**, with Large Language Models (LLMs) being the key subset focused on human language tasks including text generation, translation, and summarisation.

## 3. Guidance for GP Registrars (Trainee Portfolio)

### 3.1 Declaration Requirements

- **The RCGP does NOT require GP registrars to declare the use of generative AI in entries on the Trainee Portfolio.**
- However, it is the GP registrar's responsibility to ensure AI use is consistent with RCGP guidance and their wider professional responsibilities.
- Users bear responsibility for **"checking and verifying each output."**

### 3.2 Acceptable Uses

**Reference and Information Synthesis:**

- Gathering and summarising data from multiple sources
- Using AI as a "quick reference and information synthesis tool"
- Must be applied critically with verification of sources

**Feedback Support:**

- Identifying knowledge gaps and misunderstandings
- Supplements (but does NOT replace) supervisor feedback
- AI-generated feedback must be "reflected upon, and challenged and fact checked"

**Teaching and Learning Support:**

- Producing slides and summaries
- Creating multiple-choice questions
- Simulating cases for practice

**Reflection Support:**

- Providing prompts for personal reflection
- Suggesting relevant curriculum capabilities
- Identifying applicable patient groups
- **Cannot replace genuine reflective practice**

### 3.3 Prohibited / Unacceptable Uses

- **No artificial patient encounters:** Using AI to create fabricated patient encounters or taking "a purely mechanistic, cut-and-paste approach" to learning logs raises probity concerns.
- **Clinical Case Review entries must be based on actual patient interactions** to ensure training breadth and avoid probity issues.
- **Must NOT use generic AI tools (e.g. ChatGPT) for diagnosis generation, clinical information interpretation, or management advice** — this poses patient safety risks.
- **Generative AI should not be used to generate reflections without real patient experience.**

### 3.4 Critical Engagement Requirements

Registrars must:

- **"Critically engage with the AI generated output; question validity, verify sources and discuss discrepancies"**
- Trace information back to original sources
- Challenge AI findings when uncertain
- Ensure AI use complies with employer, deanery, and organisational data protection requirements and confidentiality policies
- Ensure any prompts used are compliant with data protection policies

### 3.5 Out-of-Scope Uses (Not Covered by This Guidance)

These uses fall outside the training guidance scope (though relevant to independent GPs):

- Transcribing notes
- Summarising patient records
- Generating referral letters

## 4. Guidance for GP Educators

- Should **"expect GP registrars to use generative AI in the ways outlined in this guidance"**
- Should use AI adoption as an opportunity to **"emphasise and promote the skills required in assessing evidence"**
- Must understand that over-reliance risks undermining competency development
- Educational Supervisors and ARCP panels should **actively question learning log entries**, particularly Clinical Case Review submissions, to verify authenticity and ensure genuine learning occurred

## 5. Portfolio Pathway Applications (Formerly CEGPR)

This is interim guidance while the GMC reviews AI's role in Portfolio Pathway applications.

### 5.1 Acceptable Uses

- Structuring documents
- Checking grammar
- Providing general guidance on reflective writing techniques

### 5.2 Critical Requirements

**Content Authenticity:** All submitted material must reflect **"the applicant's own knowledge, skills, and personal professional experiences."** AI-generated content cannot substitute for independent thinking or clinical judgment.

**Personal Contribution:** "Generative AI-generated material should not replace critical thinking, clinical judgment, or personal reflections, which are key components in demonstrating the capabilities required for independent general practice."

### 5.3 Prohibited Practices

- Submitting AI-generated evidence without clear personal contribution — this **"undermines the integrity of the application and may be considered a breach of ethical standards"**
- Including inaccuracies, biases, or plagiarised content inadvertently introduced by AI tools
- Using AI to generate clinical examples or reflections

### 5.4 Disclosure Requirement

**"Where generative AI tools have been used to support the preparation of documents, applicants should acknowledge this appropriately while ensuring that all clinical examples, reflections, and learning outcomes are authentically their own."**

> Note: This is stricter than the Trainee Portfolio guidance — Portfolio Pathway requires acknowledgement; Trainee Portfolio does not.

## 6. Appraisal Portfolio (Practising GPs / Revalidation)

### 6.1 Transparency Obligations

You **must** inform your appraiser about any AI use in appraisal preparation, including:

- Which AI model/software you used
- How you applied it
- Whether patient-identifiable information was involved

### 6.2 Key Restriction

**"You do not use AI to write reflections on learning"** — You may use AI prompts to initiate your own reflection process, but the reflection itself must be yours.

### 6.3 Permissible Applications

- Summarising journal articles for learning logs
- Gathering and synthesising information from multiple sources
- Providing reference material to reduce "hallucination" risks
- Creating prompts for personal reflection

### 6.4 Data Protection

- **Never enter patient identifiable information into web-hosted AI platforms**
- Ensure AI use complies with data protection and employment requirements across all your medical roles

### 6.5 Regulatory Context

- Check with your Responsible Officer about local AI rules
- The GMC currently lacks specific appraisal AI guidance but expects doctors to apply professional judgment and follow _Good Medical Practice_ principles

---

## 7. Known Risks and Limitations of AI (RCGP Position)

**Hallucination:** AI can "generate information that is incorrect or nonsensical, despite it sounding plausible," fabricating details or presenting invented information as true.

**Source Accuracy:** References and citations provided by AI "are not always accurate, or complete and can sometimes be completely fictional."

**No True Understanding:** AI "does not 'understand' these outputs" and "focusses on answer generation, not generating evidenced truth."

**Error Rates:** Error rates in generative AI remain poorly understood.

**Differential Attainment Risk:** Non-native English speakers may struggle to identify poor AI content, creating inequitable access to time-saving benefits.

---

## 8. GMC Framework (Good Medical Practice 2024)

While the GMC lacks specific appraisal AI guidance, the following principles from _Good Medical Practice 2024_ apply:

- Doctors are responsible for decisions they take when using new technologies like AI
- All existing professional standards continue to apply when using AI
- Doctors must discuss the use of innovative technologies with patients, including limitations and uncertainties
- Adverse incidents involving AI/software tools should be reported as with other medical devices

---

## 9. AI Literacy Requirements (All Users)

The RCGP mandates that all users of generative AI must understand:

- AI capabilities and limitations
- How to integrate AI tools into teaching
- Ethical, inclusive, and responsible implementation
- How to verify accuracy of AI outputs before relying on them
- How to trace information back to original sources

## 10. Summary: Rules by Portfolio Type

| Rule                          | Trainee Portfolio | Portfolio Pathway    | Appraisal Portfolio    |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Must declare AI use           | No                | Yes (acknowledge)    | Yes (inform appraiser) |
| AI can structure/draft        | Yes               | Yes                  | Yes                    |
| AI can write reflections      | No                | No                   | No                     |
| Must be based on real patients| Yes               | Yes                  | Yes                    |
| AI for grammar/formatting     | Yes               | Yes                  | Yes                    |
| AI for diagnosis/management   | No                | No                   | N/A                    |
| Fabricated encounters allowed | No                | No                   | No                     |
| Data protection compliance    | Required          | Required             | Required               |
